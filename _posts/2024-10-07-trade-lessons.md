Thanks to the trade posts on X, I've maintained some consistency with journaling. It is the lost trades that I want to explore in detail in this post. I will not look at specific trades, but general patterns that I've found in losing trades.
I posted on X this morning but decided to delete after watching Hancock's overview of the week. That answered some questions for me.

Some reasons for my losses last week:

## 9PM Entries
Last week, I religiously followed every 1 and 9 candle for a CRT trade. After reviewing I can see that I have not had a successful trade setup at 9PM. I only had one Gold win two weeks ago, and it was only 27 pips. This week I've decided I will not trade 9PM for all assets.

## M5 Execution Instead of M1
In the morning I decided to stick to M5 executions, since many of my losses came from early entries on M1. But the video from Hancock clarified that M1 can be the ideal execution timeframe. The missing piece for this execution is a key level. We should not consider anything a CSD until it takes out a key level. For CSD1 this can be the H4 high or low, but for CSD2 it should be a keylevel - An OB, BB, FVG, Day's High, Week's High etc.

So with that in mind, the simplest entry can be from generic CSD but that will result in a lot of stops being hit. The most accurate execution will be to base the reversal on a tap into liquidity at a key level. This is what we need to study.

## Not studying HTF Narrative and Key Levels
It can be said that Hancock's model can be executed without regard to key levels. This is true for Gold when trading at all-time highs. But the HTF order will still be critical in determining the overall move. We can use similar analysis as M5 for H4, D1, W1, even M. 

Key levels are another factor we need to train our eyes to see. Sometimes key levels will be on a lower timeframe at m15 or M30. We have to get used to this check. With this in mind, not all CSDs are valid. Only CSDs that tap into a key level are valid. Once we factor this in, the winrate should rise dramatically.

## Not taking TP at 50%
This is another mistake I made last week. 100% TP is achieved when there is strong momentum. And sometimes BE SLs will be hit first. 50% is a guarantee that should be taken at all times. Even settling for 50% CR is perfectly valid. We can factor in position sizes and risk to this expectation. So we disregard the remaining leg of the move and just exit at 50%.

## Closing 1AM Trades too Early
Last week we saw a number of US30 and US100 trades move to 100% TP over 8 hours. From 1AM to 9AM. From this, I reasoned that perhaps the trades should be left open to float with BE until 100%. There's no need to put a 25% CR SL. Let it float.

## Tight SLs
Related to the above, and also related to valid CSDs. SL placement is critical in keeping a valid winning trade open. The simplistic approach of SL after CSD2 has not been helpful for me on US30 and Gold. So for now, while studying valid CSDs, I will have larger SLs for these pairs. US100 seems to be working fine.

## Not Having a Continuation Model
The Hancock continuation model is the missing tool for higher winrate. I've noticed all 3 pairs today not doing a reversal. They seem to have consolidated at 9PM. I am waiting for the 1AM price action. We will explore this in detail, but I expect to start at least demo trading it this week.
I will keep to the reversal model for my real trades. The continuation model can be demo traded for at least 1 week.
